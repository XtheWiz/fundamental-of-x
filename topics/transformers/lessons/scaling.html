Scaling & Emergence

Bigger isn't supposed to be a research result, but in LLMs it is. Plot loss against parameters or compute on log-log axes and you get a remarkably straight line — over many orders of magnitude. Capability gains, though, are not smooth.

Scaling laws (Kaplan, Chinchilla)

Empirically, validation loss falls as a power law in: parameters (N), dataset size (D), and compute (C). Each roughly straight on a log-log plot. Chinchilla refined this: for a fixed compute budget, train a smaller model on more data than people were doing in 2020.

The rough rule: parameters and training tokens should scale together. 20 tokens per parameter is a popular ratio (Chinchilla-optimal).

Emergence

While loss falls smoothly, capabilities often appear in steps. A task the model can't do at all at 1B params suddenly works at 10B. Examples: in-context learning, chain-of-thought reasoning, basic arithmetic, code from description.

(Caveat: some "emergent" capabilities are partly an artifact of how we measure them — binary accuracy on a hard task looks like a jump even if underlying probability is rising smoothly. Don't over-mystify.)

Key takeaways

  • For a fixed compute budget, there's an optimal split between bigger model and more data. Both Kaplan and Chinchilla papers are worth reading.
  • Pre-training compute is bounded by money; inference compute is bounded by users × queries. Different scaling concerns at each stage.
  • Quality of data matters as much as quantity. Modern frontier models curate aggressively (filtering, deduplication, mix tuning) — the easy gains from "more web text" ran out around 2022.
  • Scaling will eventually hit limits — energy, hardware, training data. Whether reasoning capability also hits a ceiling is an open question.
