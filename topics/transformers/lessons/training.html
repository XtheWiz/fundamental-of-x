Lesson 06

Next-Token Training

One objective, applied a trillion times: predict the next token. Take a sequence, run it through the model, compare the predicted distribution to the actual next token, take a step of gradient descent. Repeat until the model has read most of the internet.

The loss

At every position, the model outputs a distribution over the entire vocabulary (50k–200k tokens). The training loss is cross-entropy: how surprising was the actual next token? Confident-and-right = low loss. Confident-and-wrong = high loss. The model learns to be probabilistically calibrated.

Why this is enough

"Predict the next token" sounds limited, but to do it well you need:

  • Knowledge of grammar (predict the right part of speech)
  • Knowledge of facts ("the capital of France is …")
  • Reasoning chains (predicting the next step of a proof)
  • Style imitation, code structure, math, dialogue norms

All of this is in the training data, encoded as "what token comes next?" The model has to absorb it all to lower its loss.

Try it

Watch a model predict each next token in a sentence. See per-token loss and the cumulative training signal.

Key takeaways

  • Pre-training is just this loss, on the entire internet. Modern runs see trillions of tokens, costing tens of millions of dollars in compute.
  • The model's "knowledge" emerges as a side effect of getting the next token right. No explicit knowledge graph; just weights.
  • Post-training (RLHF, DPO, etc.) refines the base model into a chat assistant by additional training on human-preferred outputs.
  • Loss is measured in nats (or bits) per token. Lower = better. Modern LLMs hit around 1.5–2.0 nats per token on diverse text.
