Sampling

A trained model gives you a probability distribution over possible next tokens. How do you pick one? "Always the most likely" is boring and gets stuck in loops. "Pick at random" generates nonsense. The art of sampling is between.

The knobs

  • Greedy — always pick the highest-probability token. Deterministic, dull.
  • Temperature — divide logits by T before softmax. T < 1 sharpens the distribution (more deterministic); T > 1 flattens it (more creative). T → 0 = greedy.
  • Top-k — only consider the k most likely tokens; sample among those.
  • Top-p (nucleus) — only consider the smallest set of tokens whose probabilities sum to p (e.g., 0.9). Adapts to the distribution's shape.

In practice they're combined: temperature=0.7, top_p=0.95 is a common default.

Try it

A made-up distribution over five plausible next tokens. Slide the knobs; watch which tokens are reachable and how the sampling probabilities reshape.

Key takeaways

  • Greedy decoding is bad for open-ended generation but fine for "best answer to a yes/no question" tasks.
  • Higher temperature = more diverse output but more risk of nonsense. Lower = more reliable but more repetitive.
  • Top-p adapts: at confident positions (peaky distribution) it samples from few options; at uncertain ones it considers more.
  • Sampling is the only stochastic part of an LLM. The model itself is deterministic — same input + same seed = same output.
