Delivery Semantics

How sure are you that a message arrived exactly once? The honest answer involves trade-offs. Brokers offer three guarantees, and each has a cost.

The three guarantees

  • At-most-once: send and forget. Maybe lost, never duplicated. Cheapest. Used for metrics, logs you can tolerate losing.
  • At-least-once: keep retrying until ack. Never lost, possibly duplicated. The default for most brokers. Requires idempotent consumers (next lesson).
  • Exactly-once: never lost, never duplicated. Hardest. Requires coordination between producer, broker, and consumer (or, more often, an idempotent app on top of at-least-once).

Why exactly-once is hard

The two generals problem says: with an unreliable network, two parties can never be certain a message both sent and processed exactly once. The producer might crash after sending; the consumer might crash before acking; an ack might be lost.

Real "exactly-once" systems (Kafka with transactions, Flink) use idempotency keys + atomic commit between offsets and output. It works, but adds latency and complexity. Most production systems use at-least-once + idempotent app code.

Key takeaways

  • At-least-once + idempotent consumer is the practical default. It gets you exactly-once effect for most workloads at a fraction of the cost.
  • "Exactly-once" in marketing usually means "we hide the duplicates via transactions/idempotency keys."
  • Pick the cheapest guarantee that meets the workload's requirements. Metrics losing 0.01% is fine; payments losing one isn't.
  • The semantics depend on producer config and consumer config. Acks=all + commit-after-success is at-least-once; acks=0 is at-most-once.
