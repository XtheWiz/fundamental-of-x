The Shockley-Queisser Limit

The Shockley-Queisser limit (1961) is the maximum efficiency a single-junction cell can ever achieve, no matter the engineering. For an ideal silicon bandgap and the solar spectrum, it's about 33%. The lab record is ~26.7%, and commercial panels do ~22%. We're closer to the ceiling than most people realize.

Where the energy goes

  • ~19% — sub-bandgap photons (too red, pass through).
  • ~33% — excess energy of above-bandgap photons (turns into heat).
  • ~13% — fundamental thermodynamic loss in extracting work.
  • ~33% — what you can actually convert into electricity (the SQ limit).

Beating SQ

The way to push past SQ is multi-junction cells: stack different bandgaps in series. Each layer catches the slice of the spectrum its bandgap fits. Lab records: 4-junction cells hit ~47%. Expensive — used in satellites and concentrator systems.

Key takeaways

  • Single-junction silicon is approaching its physical ceiling. The next 5% will be expensive.
  • Multi-junction tandem cells (e.g., perovskite on silicon) are the most active research area.
  • The SQ limit applies to one cell at AM1.5 standard sunlight. Concentrator cells (under 500× sun) have a higher limit.
  • Some loss mechanisms (thermal, optical) can be improved; the bandgap-related ones are fundamental.
