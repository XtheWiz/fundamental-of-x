The P-N Junction

Pure silicon doesn't make a solar cell by itself — there's nothing to push the freed electrons in any direction. The trick is doping: add a tiny bit of phosphorus (extra electrons → N-type) on one side, boron (missing electrons → P-type) on the other. At the boundary, a built-in electric field forms — and that's the cell's engine.

The depletion region

When N and P meet, free electrons from N drift across into P and recombine with holes. This leaves charged ions behind: positive on the N side, negative on the P side. The resulting electric field stops the drift. Now the layer is depleted of free carriers — and when a photon creates an electron-hole pair inside that field, the field pulls them in opposite directions through the external circuit.

Key takeaways

  • Without the P-N junction's built-in field, photon-generated electrons would just diffuse around and recombine.
  • Cell voltage is determined by the bandgap and the junction — typically ~0.6V for silicon.
  • Modern cells have more sophisticated structures (PERC, TOPCon, heterojunction) all built on the same principle.
  • The diode behavior of the junction also lets you check a cell — it should pass current one way only.
