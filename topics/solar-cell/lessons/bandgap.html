Bandgap

Electrons in a solid live at discrete energy levels grouped into bands. Between the filled (valence) band and the empty (conduction) band is a forbidden gap. Whether the material conducts depends on the size of that gap.

Three kinds of materials

  • Conductors (metals) — bands overlap. Electrons freely flow. Bandgap ≈ 0.
  • Insulators — bandgap > ~3 eV. Almost nothing crosses without serious help.
  • Semiconductors (silicon, GaAs, perovskite) — bandgap ~1–2 eV. Just right: a visible-light photon has enough energy to lift an electron across.

Key takeaways

  • Silicon bandgap: 1.12 eV. Corresponds to ~1100 nm light (near-IR) — anything more energetic can excite an electron.
  • Photons less energetic than the bandgap just pass through (transparent at those wavelengths).
  • Photons more energetic excite an electron but waste the excess as heat.
  • Choosing bandgap material is choosing which wavelengths to harvest.
