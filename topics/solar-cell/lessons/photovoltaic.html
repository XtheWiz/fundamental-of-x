The Photovoltaic Effect

A photon with enough energy hits a silicon atom and gives up its energy to one of the bound electrons. The electron jumps to the conduction band; the hole it leaves behind acts like a positive charge carrier. Together they are an electron-hole pair, and they're how solar cells produce current.

Why a photon, not a wave

The energy transfer is one photon to one electron. Higher intensity = more photons = more electrons, but the threshold (must be above bandgap energy) is per-photon. Brighter dim red light still cannot excite electrons if its photons are too weak. This is the photoelectric effect that won Einstein a Nobel.

Key takeaways

  • Photons below the bandgap can't lift an electron — they're transparent to the cell.
  • Photons way above the bandgap waste their excess energy as heat.
  • The sweet spot is photons just above the bandgap.
  • Different cell technologies tune this — silicon catches 400–1100 nm well; GaAs is similar; perovskites can be tuned.
