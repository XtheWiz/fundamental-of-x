NISQ, Noise & Error Correction

Today's quantum hardware is NISQ — Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum. Hundreds of qubits, error rates of ~0.1–10% per gate. Real quantum advantage on real problems is still ahead.

The noise problem

Qubits decohere — lose their quantum state — in microseconds. Gates have small errors. By the time you've done a thousand operations, the signal is mostly noise.

Error correction

The fix is wild: encode one logical qubit across many physical qubits (e.g., 1000:1 for the surface code), then continuously measure stabilizer operators to detect and correct errors without disturbing the encoded state.

Bottom line: practical fault-tolerant quantum computing needs millions of physical qubits. We have thousands.

Key takeaways

  • NISQ machines exist (IBM, Google, IonQ, others) but can't yet run useful Shor or huge Grover.
  • Quantum advantage demos (Google 2019, etc.) used contrived problems chosen to favor quantum hardware.
  • Useful fault-tolerant quantum computing is a 10–30 year horizon, depending on who you ask.
  • Interesting current work: hybrid quantum-classical algorithms, error mitigation, finding NISQ-suitable problems.
