Bits vs Qubits

A classical bit is 0 or 1 — one of two states. A qubit lives in a continuous space: a sphere. Any point on the surface is a valid qubit state. The trick is that you can only ever read a single bit out of it.

What's on the sphere

A qubit's state is a vector on the surface of the Bloch sphere:

  • North pole = |0⟩ (definitely 0)
  • South pole = |1⟩ (definitely 1)
  • Equator = equal superposition of |0⟩ and |1⟩

State = two angles (θ, φ) — latitude and longitude. Or two complex amplitudes: α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ with |α|² + |β|² = 1.

Key takeaways

  • A qubit's state is continuous — it lives on a 2-sphere, not in {0, 1}.
  • You only ever read a classical bit out, with probabilities |α|² and |β|² of getting 0 or 1.
  • Equator → fair coin. Poles → deterministic.
  • A 'quantum computer' is a thing that prepares and manipulates qubit states like this.
