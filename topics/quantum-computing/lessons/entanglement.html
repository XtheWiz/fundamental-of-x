Entanglement

Two qubits can share a state that neither qubit alone has. Measure one, and the other's outcome is instantly determined — even if they're light-years apart. It is the strangest part of quantum mechanics, and it is real.

A Bell pair

The state |Φ⁺⟩ = (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2 is the famous entangled state. If you measure the first qubit:

  • 50% chance you get 0 — and the second qubit is now |0⟩.
  • 50% chance you get 1 — and the second qubit is now |1⟩.

The outcomes are perfectly correlated, but until measurement, neither qubit had a definite value.

Why it isn't magic

You can't use entanglement to send information faster than light. The remote qubit's outcome looks random until you compare notes (which travels classically).

Bell's theorem (1964) proved no 'hidden variable' theory can explain entanglement correlations.

Key takeaways

  • Entanglement is correlation that cannot be explained by any local hidden state.
  • Used in quantum teleportation, superdense coding, and quantum key distribution.
  • Modern quantum computers entangle dozens to hundreds of qubits routinely.
  • No FTL communication — you need classical info to extract anything useful.
