Quantum Gates

Classical computing has AND/OR/NOT gates. Quantum computing has H, X, Z, CNOT. Each is a unitary matrix — a reversible rotation of the qubit state. Everything else is built from these.

Single-qubit gates

  • X (bit flip) — |0⟩ ↔ |1⟩. The quantum NOT.
  • Z (phase flip) — flips the sign of |1⟩. No effect on probabilities by itself.
  • H (Hadamard) — takes |0⟩ to (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2. The superposition-creating gate.

Two-qubit gates

CNOT (controlled-NOT): if the control qubit is |1⟩, flip the target. This is how qubits become entangled.

Key takeaways

  • All quantum gates are reversible (unitary). Classical AND/OR aren't.
  • H followed by H is identity — it's its own inverse.
  • {H, T, CNOT} is a universal gate set: any quantum computation can be built from them.
  • Real hardware has its own native gate set; compilers translate.
