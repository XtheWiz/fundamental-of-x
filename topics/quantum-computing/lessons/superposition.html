Superposition & Measurement

Before you measure, a qubit can be any mix of |0⟩ and |1⟩. During measurement, that mix randomly collapses to one or the other. The probabilities aren't a description of our ignorance — they are a property of the state itself.

The collapse

Suppose your qubit is in state α|0⟩ + β|1⟩. Measure it in the computational basis:

  • With probability |α|² you get 0, and the state collapses to |0⟩.
  • With probability |β|² you get 1, and the state collapses to |1⟩.

Once collapsed, repeated measurements give the same answer. The superposition is destroyed.

Try it

Key takeaways

  • Measurement is destructive. You cannot 'peek' at a qubit's amplitudes.
  • |α|² + |β|² = 1 is the normalization constraint — probabilities add to 1.
  • Same state, repeated measurements: the empirical distribution converges to |α|² and |β|².
  • This randomness is fundamental, not a measurement-error story.
