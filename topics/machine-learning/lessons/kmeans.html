K-means Clustering

Unsupervised learning's headliner. No labels — just points and the question "are there natural groups?" K-means answers yes (you tell it how many) with a beautifully simple iterative algorithm.

The algorithm (Lloyd's)

  1. Pick K random points as starting centroids.
  2. Assign: each data point joins the nearest centroid's cluster.
  3. Update: each centroid moves to the mean of its assigned points.
  4. Repeat 2–3 until centroids stop moving.

That's it. Converges quickly; the math is just averaging and distances.

The catches

  • You pick K. Wrong K = wrong clustering. Use the "elbow method" or silhouette score to choose.
  • Local minima. Different starting centroids can produce different clusterings. K-means++ initialization helps.
  • Spherical clusters only. Two interlocking moons → k-means fails. Use DBSCAN or spectral clustering for weirder shapes.
  • Sensitive to scale. Always normalize features first.

Key takeaways

  • K-means is the simplest clustering algorithm worth knowing. Fast, interpretable, used everywhere.
  • Outside it: DBSCAN (no K needed), hierarchical (dendrograms), Gaussian Mixture Models (probabilistic), spectral.
  • Clustering is a discovery tool, not a prediction one. Use it to explore data, then add labels by hand or with rules.
  • The same "assign + update" iteration shape shows up in many ML algorithms: EM, Lloyd's quantizer, the Floyd-Steinberg dithering algorithm. Worth recognizing.
