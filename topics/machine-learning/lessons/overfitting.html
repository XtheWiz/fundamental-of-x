Overfitting & Regularization

A model that fits the training data perfectly usually fails on new data. It memorized noise instead of learning the pattern. The sweet spot between under- and over-fitting is where ML practice actually lives.

The bias-variance tradeoff

  • High bias (underfit): model is too simple to capture the truth. Big errors on training and test.
  • High variance (overfit): model is too flexible. Memorizes training noise; bad on test data.
  • Sweet spot: complex enough to capture the signal, simple enough to ignore the noise.

The four tools

  • More data. The simplest fix — overfitting shrinks as N grows.
  • Smaller model. Fewer parameters → less capacity to memorize.
  • Regularization. Add a penalty term to the loss that punishes large weights (L1, L2). Forces the model to use only what it needs.
  • Validation + early stopping. Hold out 10–20% of data, watch its loss during training, stop when it stops improving.

Deep nets add: dropout, batch norm, data augmentation. Same idea — prevent the model from over-committing.

Try it

Fit a polynomial to noisy data. Slide the degree from 1 (line) to 12 (wiggle). Watch the train loss drop while the validation loss rises.

Key takeaways

  • Training loss shrinks monotonically; validation loss has a U-shape. The valley is your sweet spot.
  • "Big models always overfit" — not true at modern scale. With enough data, very large models generalize surprisingly well. This is the whole story of LLMs.
  • The cardinal sin: peeking at the test set while developing. Hold it out until the very end. Otherwise you've effectively trained on it.
  • Cross-validation is the systematic version: rotate the validation slice over the training data, average the results. More reliable than a single split.
