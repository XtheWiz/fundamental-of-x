CAP Theorem

Eric Brewer's famous 2000 conjecture (proven a few years later): a distributed system can guarantee at most two of Consistency, Availability, and Partition tolerance — when partitions happen. Most "CAP debates" boil down to people picking that "when."

The three letters

  • C — Consistency: every read returns the most recent write (or an error). All replicas agree.
  • A — Availability: every request gets a (non-error) response — though not necessarily the latest data.
  • P — Partition tolerance: the system keeps working even when messages between nodes are dropped or delayed.

In practice, partitions will happen (cables get cut, switches fail, regions go down). So P isn't really optional. CAP collapses to: when there's a partition, do you stay consistent (CP) or stay available (AP)?

Try it

Two replicas, one network. Tune partition severity and pick which side of CAP you favor. Then watch what reads and writes do.

Key takeaways

  • "Pick 2 of 3" is the snappy version. The accurate version: during partitions, choose C or A.
  • CP systems (etcd, Zookeeper, traditional RDBMS with synchronous replication): refuse writes when nodes can't talk. Used where correctness matters more than uptime.
  • AP systems (Cassandra, DynamoDB, most caches): keep serving on both sides of a partition, reconcile later. Used where uptime matters more than freshness.
  • PACELC extends CAP: even when there's no partition (Else), there's still a Latency-vs-Consistency tradeoff. Most systems lean one way or the other in both regimes.
