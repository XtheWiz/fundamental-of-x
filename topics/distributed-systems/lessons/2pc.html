Two-Phase Commit (2PC)

How do you make multiple databases commit a transaction together, or none at all? The textbook answer is 2PC. It works — most of the time. Its famous failure mode is what motivated everything we now use instead (Sagas, consensus, distributed transactions in modern systems).

The two phases

One coordinator, N participants.

  1. Prepare phase: coordinator sends "can you commit?" to every participant. Each one prepares locally (locks rows, writes undo log) and votes yes or no.
  2. Commit phase: if all voted yes, coordinator sends "commit." If any voted no (or didn't respond), coordinator sends "abort."

Participants who voted yes are stuck in the "prepared" state holding locks until they hear back. They cannot abort unilaterally — they promised to be ready.

The blocking problem

If the coordinator crashes after participants vote yes but before sending the commit/abort decision, participants are stuck. They hold locks, they can't progress, and they don't know whether to commit or abort. The whole system stalls until the coordinator recovers. This is the reason 2PC has fallen out of favor.

Try it

Run the protocol. Try each failure scenario — one participant votes no, one crashes mid-protocol, the coordinator crashes — and watch the outcome.

Key takeaways

  • 2PC guarantees atomic commit across nodes — all commit, or all abort, no in-between.
  • It is blocking: a coordinator crash at the wrong moment freezes participants. There's no way around this with only two phases.
  • Three-phase commit (3PC) exists but is more complex; rarely used in practice.
  • Modern systems prefer Sagas (compensating transactions) for cross-service workflows, and consensus protocols (Raft, Paxos) for replicated state.
