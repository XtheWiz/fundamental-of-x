Leader Election

Many distributed protocols are much easier if you have one designated coordinator. But who's the coordinator? And what happens when they die? Leader election is the meta-protocol: how a leaderless cluster picks one.

The shape of the problem

All nodes start as followers. They expect to hear from a leader periodically (heartbeats). If a follower doesn't hear from the leader within a random timeout, it promotes itself to candidate and asks the others for votes.

Each node votes once per term. Whichever candidate gets a majority becomes leader and starts sending heartbeats. The losers fall back to follower.

The randomized timeout is what breaks ties: in practice one candidate wakes up first and wins outright. Equal-timeout cases cause split votes — the cluster simply re-elects with a new round.

Try it

Five nodes. Kill the leader and watch the cluster pick a new one. Repeat — the next leader's identity is random (because timeouts are).

Key takeaways

  • Randomized timeouts are the magic — without them, every follower would time out simultaneously and split the vote forever.
  • A leader can serve N followers without ever asking them anything; the followers just have to hear a heartbeat. Cheap when stable.
  • An election freezes writes for the duration. Modern systems (etcd, Raft) target ~150ms elections to minimize this window.
  • Split-brain (two leaders thinking they're in charge) is the worst failure mode. Quorum-based protocols (next lesson) make it impossible.
