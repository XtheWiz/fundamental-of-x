Raft Consensus

Lamport's Paxos was technically brilliant and famously unreadable. Raft (2014) achieves the same guarantees with a design optimized for human comprehension — and that's why etcd, Consul, CockroachDB, and TiDB all run on it.

The protocol

Two roles, three rules:

  1. Clients send commands to the leader. Followers redirect.
  2. Leader appends to its own log, then AppendEntries to every follower.
  3. Once a majority of followers ack a given entry, the leader commits it and applies it to its state machine. Followers apply once they hear about the commit.

"Majority" is the magic. With 5 nodes, you need 3 acks to commit. That means at most 2 can be slow/dead and the cluster still progresses. It also means two competing leaders can never both commit an entry — they'd both need majorities, which would overlap.

Try it

Append entries to the leader. Watch them propagate, get acked, and commit once a majority agrees. Drop a follower's network and watch it fall behind, then catch up.

Key takeaways

  • Quorum-based: any majority can commit; any two majorities overlap. That's how Raft sidesteps split-brain.
  • Throughput is bottlenecked by the slowest member of the majority — typically the slowest 3 of 5. Outliers don't matter.
  • The same protocol handles leader election (last lesson) and log replication (this one). Same RPCs, different phases.
  • Raft is now the default consensus algorithm in production. Paxos lives mostly in academic papers and the deeply-customized Google bits (Spanner).
