Consistency Models

"What does a read return?" sounds like a settled question. With replicas and replication lag, it has at least four answers — and the choice is one of the most fundamental design knobs in any distributed system.

The four common models

  • Strong consistency (linearizable) — every read sees the most recent write. Simulates a single machine. Expensive: requires coordination across replicas before any read returns.
  • Read-your-writes — a client always sees its own past writes, but may see stale data from others. The bare minimum for "the UI feels responsive after I post."
  • Causal consistency — if A causes B, every observer sees A before B. Concurrent (unrelated) events may be seen in different orders. The sweet spot for many real workloads.
  • Eventual consistency — given enough time without writes, all replicas converge. Until then, anything goes. Cheapest; common in caches, CDNs, DNS.

Try it

Same scenario, four models. A timeline with 3 replicas and a few writes — see what a read at each timestamp would return under each model.

Key takeaways

  • Strong consistency is easy to reason about but expensive to provide. Every read or write may need to round-trip to a coordinator.
  • Eventual is cheap but exposes inconsistencies to users. "Why does my comment show up sometimes and not others?" is an eventual-consistency complaint.
  • Causal consistency is often the practical sweet spot — read-your-writes plus "everything you've observed stays consistent" with no global coordination.
  • Most real systems mix: strong consistency for billing, eventual for analytics, causal for feeds. Pick per-data-class.
