Memory Allocation

Every malloc asks the allocator for a chunk of heap. Every free returns it. Over time the heap looks like Swiss cheese — and that's the source of half of all memory-related performance complaints.

The allocator's job

The kernel hands out memory in large pages (typically 4 KB) to processes via brk() / mmap(). The allocator — usually a library like glibc's ptmalloc, jemalloc, or mimalloc — subdivides those pages into arbitrary-sized chunks for your malloc calls.

When you free, the chunk goes back to the allocator's free list. Adjacent free chunks may be coalesced, but holes between live chunks can't be moved without updating every pointer in your program (which the allocator doesn't have).

Fragmentation

Two kinds:

  • External fragmentation — plenty of total free space, but it's scattered in small holes. Big allocations fail despite "free memory available."
  • Internal fragmentation — your 13-byte allocation gets a 16-byte chunk (allocator rounds up). The 3 wasted bytes accumulate.

Garbage-collected languages can do compaction (move live objects to defragment). Manual-memory languages can't.

Try it

Allocate and free chunks in a small heap. Watch how fragmentation builds up — and how it limits big allocations.

Key takeaways

  • "My program uses 500 MB but the OS thinks it's using 2 GB" is almost always fragmentation. Free space inside the process is not free space to the OS.
  • Different strategies trade speed for fit quality: first-fit (fast, more fragmentation), best-fit (slower, less fragmentation), buddy (fast + bounded fragmentation, used by Linux's kernel allocator).
  • Modern allocators (jemalloc, mimalloc) use size classes and per-thread arenas to nearly eliminate contention and reduce fragmentation.
  • Tools like valgrind, heaptrack, and ASan find the leaks and the bloat — use them before reasoning about performance.
