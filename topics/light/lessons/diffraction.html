Diffraction & Interference

Light is a wave — and waves bend around obstacles and overlap. The famous double-slit experiment: shine a single beam through two slits and the screen behind shows alternating bright and dark fringes.

Why fringes?

Each slit acts as a new source. The waves from the two sources add up. Where peaks meet peaks → bright. Where peaks meet troughs → dark. The fringe spacing depends on wavelength and slit separation.

Key takeaways

  • Diffraction sets a fundamental limit on image resolution (Rayleigh criterion).
  • CDs, peacock feathers, and butterfly wings show colors via diffraction, not pigment.
  • X-ray diffraction is how the structure of DNA was discovered.
  • Even a single-particle (electron, photon, atom) shows wave behavior in the double-slit — wave-particle duality (lesson 8).
