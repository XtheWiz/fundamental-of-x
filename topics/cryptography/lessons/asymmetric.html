Asymmetric Crypto

One of the strangest, most beautiful ideas in computing: a keypair where the two halves are mathematically linked but knowing one tells you nothing about the other. Anyone can encrypt to you using your public half; only you can decrypt with the private half you never share.

The asymmetric trick

  • A keypair is generated together — one public, one private. Both halves are needed; you can't make one from scratch later.
  • Anyone with your public key can encrypt a message that only your private key can read.
  • The reverse also works (lesson 5): you can sign with your private key, and anyone with your public key can verify it.
  • The math is hard. RSA depends on factoring giant numbers; elliptic curves depend on discrete logs. Both are infeasible at current scale.

The catch

Asymmetric crypto is slow — orders of magnitude slower than symmetric. So in practice (TLS, PGP, Signal), it's only used to agree on a fresh symmetric key. The actual bulk traffic flows over fast symmetric crypto.

Try it

Generate a keypair. Encrypt a message with the public key. Decrypt with the private key. Try doing it the wrong way and watch it fail.

Key takeaways

  • Public is shoutable. Put it on a billboard. It only lets people send you stuff, not read your stuff.
  • Private is sacred. Anyone who has it can decrypt everything sent to you and sign messages as you.
  • Asymmetric is too slow for bulk data. It bootstraps the symmetric layer; that's the workhorse.
  • RSA is the classic algorithm; ECC (Elliptic Curve) is the modern one — smaller keys, same security.
