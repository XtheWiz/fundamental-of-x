Lesson 02

Symmetric Crypto

One shared key, used to lock and unlock. Symmetric ciphers are fast, well-studied, and form the workhorse of real-world encryption. The hard part isn't the math — it's getting the key into both hands without anyone else seeing.

The two parties, the one key

Alice and Bob share a secret key. Alice encrypts a message with it. Bob decrypts with the same key. Anyone watching the wire sees only ciphertext — opaque, scrambled, useless without the key.

This works beautifully if they share a key. Which leads to:

The key distribution problem. How do Alice and Bob agree on a key in the first place — over a network they don't trust? Spoiler: that's what asymmetric crypto and Diffie-Hellman solve (lessons 3 & 4).

Try it

Encrypt a message with AES-256-GCM. Watch the ciphertext. Re-encrypt — notice it's different every time. (That's the random IV doing its job.) Then decrypt back.

Key takeaways

  • AES (with GCM mode) and ChaCha20-Poly1305 are the modern symmetric defaults. Both authenticated — they detect tampering automatically.
  • Encrypting the same plaintext twice should produce different ciphertext (that's the IV/nonce). If yours doesn't, that's a bug.
  • Symmetric crypto is fast — TLS uses it for the actual data transfer. Asymmetric is only used at handshake time to agree on the symmetric key.
  • Key length matters: AES-128 is fine today; AES-256 if you want headroom against future cryptanalysis (and quantum).
