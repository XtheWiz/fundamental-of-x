Digital Signatures

Encryption hides; signatures prove. A signature lets anyone verify that this exact message came from the holder of a specific private key — and that no one has tampered with a single byte along the way.

Asymmetric, but flipped

Encryption: anyone with your public key can encrypt to you; only your private key can decrypt.

Signatures use the same keypair the other way:

  • You sign with your private key.
  • Anyone verifies with your public key.
  • Verification answers two questions in one shot: did this come from you? and has it been modified since?

Under the hood

A signature is really a hash of the message, "encrypted" with your private key (loose description; real schemes like ECDSA are more nuanced). To verify, the recipient hashes the message themselves and checks the signature with your public key. If the two hashes line up, the signature holds. If anyone changed even one byte of the message, the recipient's hash differs and verification fails.

Try it

Generate a keypair, sign a message, verify. Then change one character of the message and watch verification fail.

Key takeaways

  • Signatures provide authenticity (who signed) and integrity (unchanged since signing), simultaneously.
  • They do not provide confidentiality — the message is in plaintext alongside the signature. Combine with encryption if you need both.
  • ECDSA (elliptic curves) and Ed25519 are the modern standards. RSA signatures are still common but bulkier.
  • Used everywhere: TLS certificates, package signing (apt, npm), software updates, JWTs, git commits, blockchain transactions.
