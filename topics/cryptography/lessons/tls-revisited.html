Lesson 08

TLS Revisited

The TLS handshake makes a lot more sense once you've seen the primitives. We walked through the messages in the Internet topic's TLS lesson — now let's annotate each step with the crypto piece it uses, and see how they all fit together.

The full toolkit

Every primitive from the previous seven lessons shows up in TLS:

  • Hashing (lesson 1) — for transcript integrity (Finished message).
  • Symmetric crypto (lesson 2) — bulk encryption after handshake (AES-GCM).
  • Asymmetric crypto (lesson 3) — server's keypair, used for authentication.
  • Diffie-Hellman (lesson 4) — the ephemeral key exchange (ECDHE).
  • Digital signatures (lesson 5) — server signs the handshake to prove identity.
  • Certificate chains (lesson 6) — how the client knows it's the real server.

Password hashing (lesson 7) is the odd one out — it doesn't appear in TLS. It's the user-authentication primitive that runs on top of the encrypted channel TLS sets up.

Key takeaways

  • TLS is a protocol composition — none of the building blocks is exotic, but assembling them safely takes care.
  • Authentication (the cert + signature) is what stops MITM attacks; key agreement (DH) is what gives forward secrecy.
  • The symmetric key derived from the DH exchange does all the bulk encryption. Asymmetric crypto only happens at handshake time.
  • TLS 1.3 finished trimming legacy options. Older versions allowed many cipher suites; modern TLS narrows the choices to the ones still considered safe.
