Diffie-Hellman

The 1976 trick that made the modern internet possible: two strangers, shouting numbers across a crowded room with an eavesdropper listening to every word, end up with a shared secret no one else can compute. It's still magic to watch.

The paint analogy

The cleanest mental model: imagine paint that mixes but doesn't un-mix.

  1. Alice and Bob publicly agree on a common color (call it yellow).
  2. Each picks a secret color (Alice picks red, Bob picks blue) and never reveals it.
  3. Each mixes their secret into the yellow base → Alice gets orange, Bob gets green. They send these publicly.
  4. Each mixes their own secret into the color they received. Alice mixes red into green; Bob mixes blue into orange. Both end up with the same brownish color.
  5. An eavesdropper saw yellow, orange, and green — but without one of the secrets, they cannot un-mix to reach the final color.

The real math

The paint is an analogy. The actual operation is:

  • Public: large prime p and base g
  • Alice picks secret a, sends A = ga mod p
  • Bob picks secret b, sends B = gb mod p
  • Alice computes Ba mod p; Bob computes Ab mod p. Both equal gab mod p.
  • Eve has p, g, A, B — and computing gab from those is the "discrete log" problem, which is infeasible for large p.

Try it

Step through the exchange. Watch Eve's view stay opaque while Alice and Bob arrive at the same secret.

Key takeaways

  • Diffie-Hellman doesn't send a key; it lets both sides derive the same key from public exchanges.
  • Modern variant: ECDH (Elliptic-Curve Diffie-Hellman) — same idea, smaller keys, faster.
  • Vulnerable to man-in-the-middle: if Eve can intercept and replace the public values, she ends up in two separate exchanges. That's why TLS combines DH with authentication (certificates).
  • The "ephemeral" in TLS 1.3's ephemeral DH means a fresh keypair per session — even if a server's long-term key is compromised tomorrow, today's recordings stay secret. That's forward secrecy.
