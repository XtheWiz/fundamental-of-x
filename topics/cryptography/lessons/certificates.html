Certificate Chains

Public-key crypto solves "encrypt to this stranger" — once you have their public key. But how do you get it without an attacker swapping it for theirs? Certificates are the internet's answer: a chain of signatures back to roots that everyone agrees to trust.

The chain of trust

Three players:

  • Root CA — a small set of organizations (DigiCert, Let's Encrypt, Sectigo, …) whose root certificates ship pre-installed with your browser/OS. Self-signed; trust by convention.
  • Intermediate CA — signed by the root, signs leaf certs. Keeps the root's key cold-storage safe.
  • Leaf certificate — the actual cert for fundamentalofx.com (or whatever site). Signed by the intermediate.

When your browser connects, the server hands over the leaf cert plus the intermediate. The browser already has the root. Verification walks up the chain: leaf signed by intermediate? intermediate signed by root? root trusted? All yes → green padlock.

Try it

Pick a domain. Trace the chain. Try tampering with a cert anywhere in the chain — watch the green padlock turn into a "your connection is not private" warning.

Key takeaways

  • The internet's trust model is delegated. Every TLS site relies on the ~150 root CAs your browser ships with.
  • If a CA gets compromised (it has happened: DigiNotar, Symantec), every cert they signed becomes suspect. That's a real political/operational concern.
  • Certificate Transparency (CT logs) is a public ledger of all issued certs — so a fraudulent cert can be detected even if the CA was tricked into issuing it.
  • Let's Encrypt automated this whole process for free. That's why HTTPS is now ~95% of web traffic, up from ~30% a decade ago.
